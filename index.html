Chapter 1 — Foundation

💡 Why Debt & Equity? Introduction to Equity & Shares

Real Story: Reliance needs ₹20,000 crores to expand. Instead of a complex bank loan, it approaches the public. It can give them either ownership (equity) or a loan arrangement (debt). Both are ways to raise funds from the general public.

What is Equity?

When Reliance sells equity to the public, it offers ownership stakes in the company. External investors become shareholders — they hold a portion of Reliance's ownership. In exchange, they provide funds used for development and operations.

What is a Share?

A share = a unit of ownership in a company. Ram buys 100 shares of Reliance = Ram has purchased 100 units of ownership. Each share = a fractional ownership interest.

Capital = accounting term for the amount of money needed to run the business.
Equity capital = funds raised by offering ownership in the form of shares to investors.

11 Features of Equity

1. Issuer & Investor
Issuer = entity offering shares. Investor = person buying shares (general public).
2. Management & Control
Issuing shares distributes ownership among many shareholders. Reduces single-entity control.
3. Dilution of Control
Issuing more shares reduces existing shareholders' ownership %. Their decision-making authority decreases.
4. Dividends
Return that equity investors receive. A reward for financing the company's operations.
5. Variable Return
Dividends fluctuate based on company profitability. NOT predetermined. High profit → higher dividends, and vice versa.
6. Relationship
Issuer offers shares. Shareholder buys and holds them → becomes a partial owner of the company.
7. Voting Rights
"One share, one vote" principle. Larger shareholders have more influence. Ensures fairness in corporate governance.
8. Risk in Equity
Dividends are variable. If company doesn't generate profit, shareholders may receive no dividends → financial risk.
9. Claim over Assets
Shareholders gain ownership rights over company assets (machines, land), limited to their % shareholding.
10. Long-term Money
No fixed maturity period. Returns typically come when company dissolves/liquidates. Long-term investment.
11. Inside vs Outside Shareholders
Inside = executives, directors, employees involved in management. Outside = investors not in day-to-day management.

Share vs Stock vs Equity

Share = Ownership unit in ONE specific company (e.g., Reliance shares)
Stock = A broader portfolio of shares from MULTIPLE companies (Reliance + Adani + Bajaj = Stock portfolio)
Equity = The overall concept of ownership via financial instruments

Chapter 3 Part 1

📄 Shares & Different Types of Shares

Shares were originally issued as physical certificates signed by the company. Today, shares are traded and transferred electronically/digitally.

Share Pricing Terms

Face Value (Par Value)
Original cost printed on the share certificate. Dividend is calculated on face value.
Example: Face value ₹10, dividend declared 50% → dividend = ₹5 per share.
Issue Price
Price at which company sells shares in the primary market to the general public.
Current Market Price
Price at which shares trade on NSE/BSE (secondary market). Decided by market forces and company performance.

Chapter 3 — Preference Share Types

⭐ Types of Preference Shares

The process of issuing preference shares by a company is called a Preferential Issue.
1. Convertible Preference Shares
Can be converted into ordinary equity shares at agreed terms and conditions.
1b. Non-Convertible
NO option to convert into equity shares. Stays as preference shares only.
2. Redeemable Preference Shares
Have a maturity date on which company repays capital and stops dividends.
2b. Irredeemable Preference Shares
No maturity date. Company does not have a fixed obligation to repay capital.
3. Participating Preference Shares
Can participate in extra profits of the company APART from the fixed dividend.
3b. Non-Participating
Do NOT share in extra earnings or surplus assets during liquidation.
4. Cumulative Preference Shares
Entitled to receive ALL unpaid dividends from PAST periods + current dividend, even if company skipped payments due to insufficient profits.
4b. Non-Cumulative
Cannot claim unpaid dividends from previous years. Only current year dividend if declared.
📌 Legal Limit (Section 55, Companies Act 2013):
Preference shares: max 20 years from date of issue.
Infrastructure companies: max 30 years.
Chapter 3 — Primary Market Offerings

🎁 Bonus Issue, Rights Shares, Private Placement & ESOP

Bonus Issue (Scrip Issue / Capitalisation Issue)

An offer of free additional shares to existing shareholders. Used when company is short on cash but wants to reward shareholders. Alternative to paying cash dividends.

• No money collected from investors — NO cash flow to company.
• Shareholders can sell bonus shares to meet liquidity needs.
• Issued proportionally to each shareholder's existing stake.
• Example: 3:2 bonus → 3 shares for every 2 held. If you hold 1,000 shares → 1,000 × 3/2 = 1,500 bonus shares.

Rights Issue

An invitation to existing shareholders to buy additional new shares at a discounted price compared to market price, on a specified future date.

• Unlike Bonus Issue, shareholder must PAY for rights shares.
• Shareholder can opt in (pay and get shares) OR opt out.
• Why do companies do this? To raise more capital for expansion (can't use bonus since that gives free shares).
• Renunciation of Rights: Shareholders can decline rights and sell their rights entitlements to other investors in the open market.

Bonus vs Rights — Quick Comparison

Price paidFREEDiscounted price (below market)
Money to companyNOYES
PurposeReward shareholders (no cash)Raise capital for expansion

Private Placement

A company offers its securities to a specific, identified group of investors (e.g., financial institutions, mutual funds) — NOT the general public.

• Per Companies Act 2013: offer to not more than 200 persons.
• SEBI's public issue regulations do NOT apply.
• Offer made by name to identified persons.
• Details filed with Registrar of Companies.
• Payment only through banking channels — NOT by cash.
• Advantages: investors are informed, fewer regulations, less time-consuming, cost-efficient.

ESOP — Employee Stock Option Plan

Shares issued to the employees of the company. Recipients are called internal shareholders because they are actively involved in the company's functioning, not just investing.

Chapter 4

🚀 IPO & FPO — Offering in Primary Market (Part 2)

Stock exchanges (NSE, BSE) = platforms/middlemen where shares are bought and sold. Companies issue equity via two main routes: IPO and FPO.

IPO — Initial Public Offering

Process through which a privately held company becomes public by offering shares to the public for the FIRST TIME. Private company = only a few insiders/initial owners held shares. IPO is a primary market event (company directly issues to investors).

Listing = The process of a company getting registered on stock exchanges, which allows its shares to be traded on the open market. Happens when a company issues an IPO for the first time.

FPO — Follow-on Public Offering

A subsequent offering of additional shares by a company that is already listed on the Indian stock market. No need to re-register.

Example: Reliance lists on Jan 1, 2020 (IPO). On Jan 1, 2024, if Reliance needs more capital, it does an FPO — no new registration needed.

Fresh Issue vs Offer for Sale (OFS)

Both IPO and FPO can be structured as either Fresh Issue or Offer for Sale:

Money goes toCompany's treasuryExisting selling shareholders (promoters/early investors)

Chapter 5 — 7 Steps

🔢 Complete IPO Process (7 Steps)

1

Register on Indian Stock Market (Listing)

Company registers on NSE or BSE. Must declare the SIZE of IPO:
(A) Quantity: Number of shares to be issued — decided by Board of Directors.
(B) Value: Total number of shares × Price per share = monetary value of IPO.

2

Select a Merchant Banker (Investment Banker / Lead Manager)

Manages the entire IPO. Services include:
• Banking services: Collects money from public, transfers to company.
• Underwriting: Guarantee service (explained in Step 3).
• Pre-IPO support: Activities leading up to IPO launch.
• Post-IPO support: Helps company thrive in public markets after listing.
Also called: Lead Bank, Banker to the Issue, Lead Manager.

3

Select an Underwriter

Provides financial stability to the IPO.
• SEBI rule: Minimum 90% of issue size must be subscribed.
• If IPO is undersubscribed (below 90%), the underwriter buys the shortfall shares.
• Example: Only 84% subscribed → Underwriter buys remaining 6% → crosses 90% mark.
• If 90% is reached organically, underwriter need NOT buy.
• Generally, underwriting services are provided by the merchant banker itself.
• Undersubscription: Demand for shares < shares offered (low investor interest).
• Oversubscription: Demand for shares > shares available (strong investor interest).

4

Draft Documents (Prospectus)

A prospectus = legally mandated document with comprehensive company details — empowers investors to make informed decisions.

📄 Red Herring Prospectus: Submitted to regulatory authorities before IPO launch. Includes company finances, operations, risks. LACKS final price and share numbers. Once approved, a final prospectus with specific details is issued.

📄 Abridged Prospectus: Summary/shorter version of Red Herring Prospectus. Contains all important details in concise form.

📄 Letter of Offer: Issued ONLY when company is going for a Rights Issue. (NOT for IPO/FPO.)

5

Road Show (Marketing)

Company initiates a marketing campaign to promote awareness of the IPO. Reaches out to potential investors and creates awareness about the opportunity to invest.

6

Pricing of Securities

Two methods to determine share price:

💰 Fixed Price Issue: Company publicly declares a SET price. Example: Reliance quotes ₹100/share. Ram wants 20 shares → pays ₹2,000 (20 × ₹100). Simple and transparent.

📊 Book Building Issue: Company gives a PRICE RANGE (e.g., ₹96–₹100). Investors bid within the range at prices like ₹96, ₹97, ₹98, ₹99, ₹100.
• Lower limit = Floor Price / Lower Band
• Upper limit = Cap Price / Higher Band
• SEBI Rule: Difference between upper and lower band cannot exceed 20%.
• Example: Floor ₹100 → Cap cannot exceed ₹120.
• Final issue price = price at which HIGHEST number of bids received (market forces decide).

7

Selling of Securities (Allotment)

Company finalizes sale of shares. Shares allocated to investors. Investors receive shares and make payments to the issuing company.

Chapter 6 — Indian Stock Market

📊 Indian Stock Market — Regulation Side

SEBI (Securities and Exchange Board of India) = Regulatory body for the capital market and equity market in India. Has power to regulate and penalize registered stock markets and stockbrokers.

Key Laws Governing Indian Capital Market

A. SEBI Act, 1992
Gave SEBI statutory powers for:
• Protecting investor interests
• Promoting securities market development
• Regulating the securities market
B. Securities Contracts (Regulation) Act, 1956
Regulates securities trading and stock exchanges. Prevents undesirable transactions. Central Govt has regulatory jurisdiction over stock exchanges, contracts, and listings.
C. Depositories Act, 1996
Facilitates establishment of depositories. Enables dematerialization of securities. Makes securities freely transferable. Electronic transfer of ownership — no physical movement needed. Eliminates transfer deeds.
D. Prevention of Money Laundering Act (PMLA), 2002
Primary objective: Prevent money laundering and provide for confiscation of property derived from money laundering. Money laundering = acquiring, owning, possessing or transferring proceeds of crime.
IOSCO = International Organization of Securities Commissions. Global body that sets standards for regulating securities markets — ensures fairness, transparency, and investor protection worldwide. Promotes cooperation among securities regulators across countries.

Chapter 7 — Misc Concepts

👥 Types of Investors in IPO

A. QIB — Qualified Institutional Buyers

Big investors perceived to have expertise and financial muscle to evaluate and invest in stock markets. IPO allocation: 50% of issue size.

QIBs include:
• Public financial institutions (Section 4A, Companies Act 1956)
• Scheduled commercial banks
• Mutual funds and Alternate Investment Funds
• Foreign Portfolio Investors registered with SEBI
• Multilateral and bilateral development financial institutions
• Venture capital funds registered with SEBI
• Foreign Venture Capital Investors registered with SEBI
• State Industrial Development Corporations
• Insurance companies registered with IRDA
• Provident Funds with minimum corpus of ₹25 crores
• Pension Funds with minimum corpus of ₹25 crores
• National Investment Fund set up by Government of India
• Insurance funds set up by army, navy, air force, and Department of Posts
• Systemically Important Non-Banking Financial Institutions

Anchor Investors (Sub-category of QIB)

• Concept introduced by SEBI on June 18, 2009
• Purpose: Enhance issuer's ability to sell issue, generate retail investor confidence, better price discovery
• Subscribe to issue BEFORE its public opening
• Pay 25% upfront margin, remaining 75% within 2 days of closure
• Lock-in periods: 30 days (50% portion) + 90 days (50% portion)
• Min investment (updated): ₹5 crore (reduced from ₹10 crore)
• Revised reservation: 40% of anchor portion reserved — 33.33% for Mutual Funds + 6.67% for Insurance Companies and Pension Funds. Unsubscribed 6.67% reallocated to Mutual Funds.
• Revised cap on anchor investors: Up to 15 investors for first ₹250 crore + 10 additional investors per subsequent ₹250 crore.

B. Retail Individual Investors (RII)

Individual investors who invest smaller amounts for personal investment. IPO allocation: 35% of issue size.

• Invest less than ₹2 lakhs to qualify as retail investor.
• Investing ₹2 lakhs or more → classified as NII/HNI, not retail.
• Retail investors can invest only at the cut-off price (price decided by issuing company).

C. NII — Non-Institutional Investors (HNI / Corporate Bodies)

• Invest more than ₹2 lakhs in IPO.
• IPO allocation: 15% of issue size.
• Sit between QIBs and Retail investors.
Example allocation: If Reliance IPO size = ₹50 crores:
QIBs: 50% = ₹25 crore | Retail: 35% = ₹17.5 crore | NII/HNI: 15% = ₹7.5 crore

Chapter 7 — ASBA & Regulation

🔒 ASBA & IPO Regulations

ASBA — Application Supported by Blocked Amount

Process to apply for IPOs. Investor's money stays in their bank but is temporarily blocked.
→ Shares allotted: blocked amount debited.
→ Less shares allotted (oversubscription): only allotted amount debited, rest unblocked.
Introduced: 2008 by SEBI | Mandatory for retail investors: 2016
Banks offering ASBA: SCSB = Self-Certified Syndicated Banks
UPI added as payment mode: 2018 (speeds up listing from 6 days → 3 days after issue closure)
UPI = Instant payment system by NPCI (National Payments Corporation of India)
Pro-rata Allotment: In oversubscription, shares allotted proportionately to each investor based on their subscription amount relative to total demand. Example: Wanted 50 shares but only 30 allotted → refund for 20 shares (under old system, now ASBA handles this).

Chapter 7 — Global Finance

🌍 Depository Receipts (DRs)

Problem: Dual listing is NOT allowed in India. Reliance's shares listed on NSE/BSE CANNOT be directly listed on foreign stock exchanges.
Solution: Depository Receipts (DRs) — financial instruments that REPRESENT shares of a local company but are listed and traded on a stock exchange OUTSIDE the country. DRs are issued in foreign currency.

Types of Depository Receipts

TypeFull NameWhere TradedCurrency
ADRAmerican Depository ReceiptUS financial markets ONLY (NYSE, NASDAQ)US Dollars
GDRGlobal Depository ReceiptAll international exchanges EXCEPT USAForeign currency of that country
IDRIndian Depository ReceiptIndian markets (for FOREIGN companies to raise capital in India)Indian Rupees

Process of Issuing ADRs (Same process for GDR, IDR)

1

Sign Agreement with Foreign Bank

e.g., Reliance signs an agreement with Bank of America (BoA) to issue ADRs on NYSE.

2

Local Subsidiary (Custodian Bank) Buys Shares

BoA's Indian subsidiary (the custodian bank) buys Reliance shares from Indian markets. e.g., buys 10 lakh shares.

3

ADRs Issued on NYSE (Conversion Ratio)

Bank of America issues ADRs on the New York Stock Exchange based on an agreed ratio.
e.g., 1 ADR = 10 Reliance shares → 10 lakh shares = 1 lakh ADRs issued on NYSE.

📌 Key Points:
• Custodian bank = local subsidiary of foreign bank operating in India
• DR holders receive dividends ONLY — NO voting rights
• Conversion ratio is decided between the companies
• 1 ADR does NOT necessarily equal 1 share — depends on agreed ratio
Chapter 7 — Foreign Investment

💱 FDI, FPI & Participatory Notes

FDI vs FPI

FeatureFDI (Foreign Direct Investment)FPI (Foreign Portfolio Investment)
NatureInvestment in business interests or physical assets in another countryInvestment in financial assets (stocks, bonds) in a country's financial markets
ControlAcquires management/operational controlNo management control
StabilityLong-term, stableShort-term, can be volatile ("hot money")
FormFactories, land, physical operationsSecurities — stocks, bonds

Downstream Investment (Indirect FDI)

Direct FDI: Mr. Anchit (USA) directly invests in EduTap (India) → this is FDI.

Downstream Investment (Indirect FDI): Mr. Anchit invests in XYZ Company (registered in India). XYZ then invests that money in EduTap.
→ The investment reaches EduTap through an Indian intermediary = Downstream/Indirect FDI.

Participatory Notes (P-Notes / PNs)

Who needs P-Notes: People living OUTSIDE India who are neither NRIs nor Persons of Indian Origin — they CANNOT invest directly in Indian markets.

How it works:
1. Such persons must invest through an FPI (Foreign Portfolio Investor) registered with SEBI.
2. FPI takes their money, invests in Indian markets, and issues P-Notes to them.
3. Value of P-Notes is derived from the underlying Indian shares bought by FPI.
→ P-Notes act like derivatives on Indian shares.

Cannot be issued to: Indian Nationals, Persons of Indian Origin (PIOs), NRIs — to prevent money laundering routes.
Chapter 7 — Miscellaneous

🔧 Green Shoe, Demutualization & Delisting

Green Shoe Option (GSO)

Used to provide price stability in secondary market immediately after listing.
• Company can allot additional shares up to 15% of issue size to public subscribers.
• Proceeds from this extra allotment kept in a separate bank account.
• If price falls BELOW issue price after listing → proceeds used to BUY shares from secondary market → supports price.
• Price stabilization period: 30 days from listing date.
• Stabilization activity done by an appointed entity — usually the lead manager (merchant banker).

Demutualization of Stock Exchanges

Process by which a member-owned stock exchange becomes a shareholder-owned stock exchange.
• Ownership changes from a few rich members (brokers, traders) → broader ownership by institutional investors, private investors, and the public.
• Applicable ONLY to stock exchanges.

Delisting of Securities

De-listing = stocks no longer listed on the stock exchange. Two types:

Chapter 7 — Social Stock Exchange

🌱 Social Stock Exchange (SSE)

Special segment of the stock market regulated by SEBI, where social organizations (charities, non-profits, for-profit social enterprises) can raise funds for projects that help society. Operates under BSE SSE and NSE SSE.
Organizations must demonstrate social intent. To qualify, an NPO or FPE must have at least 67% of revenue, expenditure, or beneficiaries linked to one or more of SEBI's 16 social activity areas.

SEBI's 16 Social Activity Areas

1. Eradicating hunger, poverty, malnutrition, inequality
2. Promoting health care, sanitation, safe drinking water
3. Promoting education, employability, livelihoods
4. Gender equality, empowering women & LGBTQIA+ groups
5. Environmental sustainability, climate change, wildlife conservation
6. Protecting national heritage, art, culture
7. Training for rural, national, Paralympic & Olympic sports
8. Supporting social enterprise incubators
9. Strengthening non-profit ecosystem (fundraising, capacity building)
10. Livelihoods for poor, small farmers, non-farm workers

Chapter 2 — Debt Market

💳 Introduction to Debt

Simple Story: Mohan borrows ₹10,000 from Sohan. Mohan = Borrower. Sohan = Lender. Mohan must repay ₹10,000 (principal) + interest. This is DEBT. The company does NOT give up any ownership when it borrows — unlike equity.

3 Steps of Debt Process

1

Issue of Debt

Reliance issues debt instruments to the public. Gets funds WITHOUT giving up ownership. Debt is quantified in monetary terms (unlike equity in number of shares).

2

Payment of Interest

Reliance pays interest on the borrowed amount. Interest = cost of using the funds. Fixed rate — unlike variable dividends in equity.

3

Repayment of Principal

At maturity, Reliance returns the principal to the investor. Debt has a defined maturity/expiry period — equity has no maturity.

8 Features of Debt

1. Debt Capital
Funds raised by borrowing (loans, bonds). Not by issuing shares.
2. Relationship
Borrower (company) and Lender. Lender is also called Investor and Creditor — holds financial claim.
3. Tradability
Certain debt instruments CAN be bought/sold on stock exchanges (BSE, NSE).
4. Fixed Rate of Return
Debt instruments usually provide FIXED interest payments. Also called fixed-rate instruments.
5. No Management Participation
Lenders have NO say in management or control of the borrowing company.
6. Priority in Repayment
In bankruptcy/liquidation, debt holders are repaid BEFORE equity shareholders.
7. Types
Bonds, debentures, commercial paper, T-bills, CDs, etc.
8. Less Risk
Less risky for investors. Regular fixed interest, set timeline for principal repayment.

4 Factors for Choosing Debt vs Equity

1. Ability to Pay Interest
Stable profits = Debt suitable (can meet regular interest obligations).
2. Willingness to Dilute Ownership
Don't want to reduce ownership stake? Choose Debt.
3. Time Period Required
Short-term capital needs = Debt. Working capital commonly funded by debt/bank borrowings.
4. Risk Tolerance
Low risk tolerance = Debt may burden them. High risk tolerance = Equity shares risk with investors.

Chapter 2 — Long-term Debt

📜 Long-term Debt Instruments

Long-term debt instruments have maturities exceeding one year. Includes bonds, debentures, and other long-maturity securities.

Government G-Secs

G-Sec = Government Security — Financial instruments issued by government to raise money.

Central Government: Can issue short-term (T-Bills, CMBs) AND long-term (Bonds/Dated Securities).
State Government: Can issue ONLY long-term securities = State Development Loans (SDL).

Bonds vs Debentures

In informal language bonds and debentures mean the same. In finance, there is a clear difference:

Capital Formation

Movement of money from investors/general public to companies for expanding operations = Capital Formation. It is the core purpose of the capital market.
Chapter 2 — Special Bonds

FCCB — Foreign Currency Convertible Bonds

• Foreign currency (usually USD) denominated debt raised by Indian companies in INTERNATIONAL markets.
• Option to convert into equity shares of the SAME company — at MATURITY.
• Interest and principal repaid in FOREIGN CURRENCY.
• Hybrid instrument — starts as debt, can become equity.
• Regulated by RBI under FEMA (Foreign Exchange Management Act).

FCEB — Foreign Currency Exchangeable Bonds

• Very similar to FCCB but KEY DIFFERENCE: debt converts to equity of a DIFFERENT company.
• E.g., XYZ issues bonds in USA → at maturity, bondholders get shares of PQR (a different company).
• Both interest and principal in foreign currency.

Chapter 2 — Short-term Govt Instruments

🏛️ Treasury Bills (T-Bills) & Cash Management Bills (CMBs)

Issued by Central/Union Government ONLY via RBI. State governments CANNOT issue T-Bills.

T-Bill Key Features

3 Maturity Periods
91 days, 182 days, 364 days. Always less than 365 days.
Issued at Discount
Issue price < Face value. No periodic interest = Zero Coupon Bill. Return = Face Value minus Issue Price.
Min Denomination
₹10,000 and multiples thereof.
Low Risk / Safe
Issued by RBI on behalf of govt. Backed by full faith of government. Virtually risk-free.
SLR Eligible
T-Bills count for Statutory Liquidity Ratio (SLR) purposes.
Settlement
Auctions every Wednesday. Settlement on T+1 basis.
Yield Formula
(Face Value − Issue Price) / Issue Price × 100. Expressed as %.
Tradable
Actively traded on Indian financial markets. High liquidity.

3 Types of T-Bills (Historical)

A. On-Tap Bills
Issued for immediate cash needs without prolonged process. Discontinued April 1, 1997.
B. Ad Hoc T-Bills
Initiated 1955. Maintained govt cash balance with RBI. Abolished 1997.
C. Auctioned T-Bills
Current method. RBI collects bids from investors. Sub-types: Yield-Based Auction and Price-Based Auction.
Competitive vs Non-Competitive Bidding:
Retail/general investors can participate on non-competitive basis. Capped at 5% of total issue. Min bid: ₹10,000. In GoI dated securities, single retail bid ≤ ₹2 crore.

Cash Management Bills (CMBs)

• Same generic character as T-Bills — issued at discount, redeemed at face value.
• Used when govt needs funds for LESS THAN 91 days.
• If need is 91 days or more → use T-Bills instead.
• Settlement: T+1 basis.
Chapter 2 — Corporate Short-term Debt

🏦 CD, Commercial Paper, Commercial Bill & ICD

Certificate of Deposit (CD)

A time deposit offered by banks/FIs. Bank acknowledges deposit and returns principal + interest at maturity.

Who can issue CDs: Scheduled Commercial Banks, Regional Rural Banks, Small Finance Banks, AIFIs (like NABARD).
Min Amount
₹5 lakh and multiples thereof.
Banks Maturity
Min 7 days, Max 1 year.
FIs/AIFIs Maturity
Min 1 year, Max 3 years.
Loans against CD
Loans CANNOT be granted against CDs. Banks CAN buy back CDs before maturity.
Format
Issued in DEMAT form. Investors can request physical form (promissory note).
Settlement
Issued on T+1 basis. Details reported to F-TRAC (Clearcorp) by 5:30 PM on day of issuance.
Negotiable vs Non-Negotiable CDs: Non-negotiable = cannot be sold before maturity. Negotiable = can be sold in secondary market before maturity. FIMMDA prescribes standard procedures in consultation with RBI.

Commercial Paper (CP)

Short-term unsecured money market instrument by corporate borrowers. Introduced in India in 1990. Also called unsecured promissory note — no collateral, only excellent credit-rated firms can issue it.

Eligibility
Companies, Primary Dealers, AIFIs. Min net worth ₹100 crore. Co-operative societies and LLPs with net worth ₹100 crore may also issue CPs.
Maturity
Min 7 days, Max 1 year from date of issue.
Min Credit Rating
A3 as per SEBI symbols. From SEBI-registered CRA.
Denomination
₹5 lakh or multiples thereof. Min investor investment = ₹5 lakh.
Always at Discount
Always issued at discount to face value.
3 Parties
Issuer (company) + IPA (Issuing and Paying Agent) + Credit Rating Agency.

Commercial Bill & ICD

Commercial Bill: Company A sells goods to Company B on credit. Company A (Drawer) issues Trade Bill to Company B (Drawee). Company A needs cash now → sells trade bill to bank at discount = bank now owns it = Commercial Bill. This = Discounting of Bills.
Demand Bill = payable on demand. Usance Bill = payable after specified future date.
ICD (Intercorporate Deposit): Unsecured borrowing by corporates from OTHER corporate entities. No bank involved. Governed under Companies Act. Higher interest than CDs (uncollateralised). Most common tenure: 90 days.
Chapter 2 — Interbank Borrowing

📞 Call Money Market & CBLO

When banks borrow from one another = Call Money. Used to meet reserve requirements (SLR, CRR). Non-banking entities CANNOT use Call Market. No collateral required. Highly liquid but also highly volatile.

3 Types of Call Markets

TypeDuration
Call Money Market1 day (overnight). Weekends/holidays excluded.
Notice Money Market2 to 14 days
Term Money MarketMore than 14 days (usually 3 months to 1 year)
Who can participate: All Scheduled Commercial Banks, RRBs, Cooperative Banks (excl. Land Development Banks), Primary Dealers, Payment Banks, Small Finance Banks.
Non-banking institutions (other than Primary Dealers) = NOT permitted.
CCIL Platform: NDS-Call (Negotiated Dealing System – Call Market). Call Rate = interest rate in call market.

CBLO — Collateralized Borrowing and Lending Obligation

For non-bank entities who CANNOT use Call Market. Operated by CCIL. Borrowers provide eligible securities (T-Bills, govt securities) as collateral to CCIL.
Maturity: 1 day to 1 year. Discounted instrument.
Key difference from Call Market: CBLO requires COLLATERAL. Call Market does NOT.
CBLO discontinued November 2018 — replaced by Triparty Repo.
Chapter 2 — Liquidity Management

🔄 Repo & Reverse Repo Transactions

Main objective = Manage liquidity (money supply) in the economy. Optimal = Moderate liquidity. Too much = inflation. Too little = economic slowdown.

REPO — Repurchase Option

Banks (e.g., SBI) sell securities (collateral) to RBI → receive cash → pay interest at Repo Rate → at maturity repurchase securities + repay principal.
Purpose: INJECT liquidity. Lower repo rate = banks borrow more = more money in economy.

Two Legs of Repo

LegBank (Borrower)RBI (Lender)
First LegSells securities, receives cashBuys securities, pays cash
Second LegRepurchases securities, pays principal + interestReturns securities, receives repayment
Also called Ready Forward Transaction — sells on spot (ready) basis, repurchases on forward basis. Repo period: Min 1 day, Max 1 year.

Eligible Collateral for Repo

Central/State Govt Securities
T-Bills & CMBs
Listed Corporate Bonds & Debentures
Commercial Papers (CPs)
Certificate of Deposits (CDs)
Units of Debt ETFs

Haircut & Types of Repo

Haircut: Amount held back by RBI. E.g., SBI gives collateral worth ₹1,000 → RBI gives only ₹900 loan → keeps ₹100 as haircut. Safety net if borrower defaults.
Overnight Repo
1-day maturity.
Term Repo
Specified maturity of more than 1 day.
Open Repo
No specified maturity date.

REVERSE REPO

COMPLETE OPPOSITE of Repo. RBI borrows from banks. Banks lend surplus funds to RBI → RBI provides G-Secs as collateral → pays interest at Reverse Repo Rate.
Purpose: WITHDRAW excess liquidity. Reduces money supply to control inflation.
Memory Trick: REPO = RBI lends to banks = Injects liquidity. REVERSE REPO = RBI borrows from banks = Withdraws liquidity.
Chapter 2 — Advanced Liquidity Tools

🏗️ SDF, MSS, Triparty Repo, LTRO & TLTRO

SDF — Standing Deposit Facility

Purpose: Withdraw excess liquidity (same as Reverse Repo) but NO collateral required.
In Reverse Repo: RBI provides G-Secs as collateral. In SDF: Banks just deposit funds with RBI directly.
Introduced by RBI on April 8, 2022. Banks earn interest on deposits under SDF.

MSS — Market Stabilization Scheme

Withdraws excess liquidity by selling government securities. Introduced: April 2004.
Securities = Market Stabilisation Bonds (MSBs). Owned by government, issued by RBI.
Money obtained kept in FIXED account — govt CANNOT use it (else liquidity re-enters economy).
Govt allocates budget to RBI annually to pay interest on MSBs.

Triparty Repo (Replaced CBLO Nov 2018)

3 parties: Borrower (needs funds, pledges securities) + Lender (provides cash) + Mediator/Triparty Agent (neutral — manages collateral, settlement).
Why CBLO replaced: CBLO allowed private bonds as collateral. Triparty Repo = only G-Secs = higher quality collateral.

Chapter 2 — RBI & Debt Market

🔁 RBI Retail Direct, NDS-OM, E-Kuber & CCIL

RBI Regulation of Debt Market

Money Market
RBI regulates entire money market. Settlement on stock exchanges = SEBI.
Long-term Govt Debt
RBI regulates G-Secs market.
Long-term Corporate Debt
Long-term corporate debt (debentures etc.) = regulated by SEBI, not RBI.

RBI Retail Direct Scheme

One-stop platform for individual investors to invest in G-Secs. Open Retail Direct Gilt (RDG) Account with RBI.
Works in both PRIMARY market (buy new G-Secs directly) and SECONDARY market (trade with other investors).
Only Government Securities — no private securities (debentures etc.).

NDS-OM, E-Kuber & CCIL

NDS-OM
Negotiated Dealing System – Order Matching. Secondary market for G-Secs. Matches buy/sell orders. Launched August 2005. Owned by RBI, administered by CCIL.
E-Kuber
Primary market platform — investors submit bids for new G-Sec issues. Also Core Banking Solution (CBS) of RBI — connects with banks 24x7. Owned and maintained by RBI.
CCIL
Clearing Corporation of India Limited. Set up April 2001. Central Counterparty (CCP). Process = Novation. Manages: NDS-Call + NDS-OM + Triparty Repo.
Chapter 2 — Debt Regulations

📋 Regulations for Debt Issue & Primary Dealers

Public Issue: Securities issued to a larger number of people. SEBI regulates private securities (debentures). G-Secs regulated by RBI.
Private Placement: Max 200 selected people.

Key Rules for Public Issue of Debt Securities

Eligibility
Public limited company under Companies Act 2013. Unlisted companies can also issue debentures and list them.
Base Issue Size
Minimum ₹100 crore. Can retain oversubscription up to 100% of base issue size.
Offer Document
Filed with stock exchange. Public comments for 7 days. Final document filed with RoC and SEBI.
Listing
Debentures MUST be listed on a stock exchange. In-principle approval needed before filing offer document.
Credit Rating
Min one CRA required. If multiple ratings obtained, ALL must be disclosed.
Min Subscription
75% of base issue size. If not received → refund within 12 days from closure.
Debenture Trustees
Must be appointed (banks/FIs registered with SEBI). Oversee investor interests.

Primary Dealers & Holding of G-Secs

Primary Dealers: RBI-registered entities licensed to buy/sell G-Secs. Buy from RBI (primary) → resell to investors (secondary) = CREATE market for G-Secs.
SGL Account
Subsidiary General Ledger. Maintained with RBI by banks, PDs, FIs. All G-Sec transactions go through SGL.
CSGL Account
Constituent SGL (SGL II). For entities not eligible for SGL. Open Gilt Account with bank/PD who maintains CSGL with RBI.
Demat Account
With NSDL/CDSL. Facilitates trading of G-Secs on stock exchanges.
Chapter 2 — Reference Rates & Misc

🌍 LIBOR, SOFR, STRIPS, NCD & Warrants

LIBOR = London Interbank Offered Rate. Global benchmark for interbank short-term lending rates. Used for international inter-bank borrowings (less than 1 year). MIFOR (Mumbai Interbank Forward Outright Rate) was calculated based on LIBOR.
2013 LIBOR Scandal: Financial irregularities found. MIFOR impacted too. RBI ruled: Indian banks going for international borrowings CANNOT use LIBOR, MIFOR, or any LIBOR-dependent rate.
Replacement: RBI focused on SOFR (Secured Overnight Financing Rate). India now uses Modified MIFOR (MMIFOR) — based on SOFR — for international borrowing calculations.

Miscellaneous Concepts

Warrants
Securities giving holder the RIGHT (not obligation) to buy securities at a set price before a set time. Usually attached to newly issued bonds or preferred stock. E.g., bond + right to buy 100 shares at $20 for 5 years.
STRIPS
Separate Trading of Registered Interest and Principal of Securities. Strips a coupon-bearing security into multiple zero-coupon securities tradable separately. E.g., 10-yr G-Sec → 1 principal + 11 coupon payment components.
NCDs
Non-Convertible Debentures. CANNOT convert to equity. Fixed/floating coupon. Min denomination ₹5 lakh. Tenor: 90 days to 1 year. Individual subscription capped at 25%. Eligible: companies, NBFCs, InvITs, REITs, AIFIs (net worth ≥ ₹100 crore).

💳 Debt Market — Quick Reference Cards

📜 Debt vs Equity

  • Equity: Ownership, Dividends, Variable return, No maturity
  • Debt: Borrowing, Interest, Fixed return, Defined maturity
  • Debt = Charge against profits (mandatory). Equity = Appropriation (discretionary).
  • In liquidation, Debt repaid FIRST before equity

🏛️ T-Bills vs CMBs

  • T-Bills: 91, 182, 364 days. Central Govt only.
  • CMBs: Less than 91 days. Same issuer (Central Govt).
  • Both issued at discount, redeemed at face value
  • Both = Zero coupon instruments. SLR eligible.
  • Settlement: T+1 basis

📑 CD vs CP

  • CD: Issued by banks/FIs. Min Rs 5L. 7 days to 1 yr (banks), 1 to 3 yr (FIs).
  • CP: Issued by corporates. Min Rs 5L. 7 days to 1 yr. Unsecured.
  • Both issued at discount. Demat form.
  • CP needs min A3 rating. CD needs no rating.

🔄 Repo vs Reverse Repo vs SDF

  • Repo: Banks borrow from RBI. Injects liquidity. Uses G-Secs as collateral.
  • Reverse Repo: RBI borrows from banks. Withdraws liquidity. Uses G-Secs as collateral.
  • SDF: RBI absorbs funds from banks. Withdraws liquidity. NO collateral needed.

💻 RBI Platforms Summary

  • E-Kuber: Primary market bids + CBS of RBI
  • NDS-OM: Secondary market G-Secs (owned by RBI, managed by CCIL)
  • NDS-Call: Call market transactions (managed by CCIL)
  • RBI Retail Direct: Individual investors buy/sell G-Secs

🌐 FCCB vs FCEB

  • FCCB: Converts to equity of SAME company at maturity
  • FCEB: Converts to equity of DIFFERENT company before maturity
  • Both: Foreign currency denominated, Hybrid instruments
  • Both regulated by RBI under FEMA
